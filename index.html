Under 184 pseudonyms, Columbia is the largest private landowner in New York City

April 20, 2023

A century after the introduction of Columbia’s fight song, the University has become not only an institution of academic prestige, but a giant of New York City real estate. The extent of the University’s institutional property holdings has, however, remained largely unclear—until now.

In the highest of published estimates, the RealDeal, a news outlet focused on commercial and residential real estate, reported in 2022 that the University owned 216 buildings in New York City, totaling 13.9 million square feet in ownership. This estimate earns Columbia the title of the largest private landowner in New York City by number of addresses. Yet, it still grossly underestimates the true size of the University’s institutional real estate portfolio beyond holdings listed under “The Trustees of the University of Columbia.”

A Spectator analysis of publicly available Accounting and Reporting at Columbia documents found that under 184 pseudonyms, Columbia has property claims on at least 383 distinct addresses.

While 263 of the ARC-identified buildings have permits under one of 33 variations of “The Trustees of the University of Columbia,” the University’s real estate claims also include properties registered with the city under 158 unrelated names, such as “The Morningside Heights Housing Corporation,” “265 River Owners Corp,” and “Hamilton Heights Cluster Associates L.P.”

ARC, a resource planning system that “gives the University the ability to manage and track all of its diverse financial activities,” places Columbia more than 250 properties above peer institutions in New York City in terms of institutional real estate. New York University and Fordham University trail behind with 119 and 25 properties respectively, according to the New York City Department of City Planning Primary Land Use Tax Lot Output.

A University spokesperson did not comment at the time of publication.

The largest of the University’s recent acquisitions are related to the construction of the Manhattanville campus, a $6 billion development spanning from 125th Street to 134th Street. Fifteen years into construction, University President Lee Bollinger characterized the development as “not even a third” complete in a November interview with Spectator.

[Read More: Bollinger’s tenure accentuated a contentious, decadeslong history between Columbia and West Harlem, some residents say]

To balance the potential displacement imposed by Bollinger’s keystone campus expansion into Manhattanville, the University collaborated with West Harlem community leaders to create the Community Benefits Agreement, a 50-page document outlining the precise obligations Columbia holds to the neighboring community. These commitments—including $4 million in legal assistance, $20 million in affordable housing, and $76 million in other resources to be administered by the West Harlem Development Corporation—were based on the “Project Site Map” illustrating the physical boundaries of the lot necessary for the new campus. According to the CBA, the University intended on building “approximately 6.8 million square feet of space,” over the document's 25-year plan.

But beyond the immediate bounds of the burgeoning Manhattanville campus, currently the site of the Columbia Business School and School of the Arts, Spectator found that the University claims an additional 16 properties in the neighborhood—totaling 713,037 square feet beyond the outlined Project Site.

“[The CBA is] a commitment we have always handled with the utmost seriousness and respect,” a University spokesperson wrote in a previous statement to Spectator. “We have never violated any aspect of the agreement and we are exceedingly proud of all that it has accomplished through our partnership with the WHDC and the communities of Upper Manhattan.”

According to the ARC reports, Columbia’s property claims, both residential and commercial, span far beyond the communities the University has entered a formal agreement with. Outside of the Manhattanville, Morningside Heights, and Medical School campuses, the University’s institutional real estate includes an additional 239 properties.

Under “institutional real estate,” the ARC report identified a diverse array of properties, including 17 Smith Street, the site of The Brooklyn Tabernacle church, and 99 Fort Washington Avenue, a public housing development in Washington Heights.

As the University continues to acquire institutional real estate and progress toward the CBA’s 6.8 million square foot development goal, some local resident organizations face challenging negotiations with Columbia.

[Read more: MHCC proposes Columbia maintain and promote affordable housing in the neighborhood in inconclusive meeting with University officials]

The Morningside Heights Community Coalition has been locked in a yearslong struggle for affordable housing inclusion within Columbia’s real estate acquisitions—and recent talks have seen little compromise from the University.

Former MHCC president Laura Friedman said she hopes to see improved consideration for local residents in Columbia’s land use decisions. Most recently, the University began converting 611 W. 112th Street into Columbia’s newest undergraduate residence hall without plans for affordable housing for local residents.

“You know, we’ve been in a lot of fights with Columbia over the years, believe me, you win a few, you lose a lot,” Friedman said.
